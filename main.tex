\begin{abstract}
Safety-critical requirements specifications should be readable, unambiguous and comprehensible. Likewise, the safety-critical software should be analyzed rigorously to assure its conformance to the specifications, and in particular the functional and timing correctness. In distributed computing, e.g., electrical/electronic vehicular system, the reliability of the safety-critical system should be maximized due to higher riks of failures which requires additional computation, and critical system resrouces such as power and energy. In order to accommodate current and feature software functionality, the distributed safety-critical software should be mapped to hardware efficiently while meeting the timing and reliability requirements.

In this thesis, we propose formal methods and optimization techniques to assure improved quality of requirements specifications and software design, and to efficiently map software functionality to hardware. Contributions of the thesis are: (i) a constrained and domain specific language of embedded systems requirement \textit{ReSA}; (ii) transformation of the software design, which is modeled in Simulink, to a network of stochastic timed automata via design patterns, and analysis of the latter using statistical model checking; (iii) power-effcient mapping of software to hardware while preserving the timing and reliability constraints of the software via integer-linear programming and meta-heuristic. Our proposed solutions are evaluated on automotive use cases such as the adjustable-speed limiter (ASL) and the brake-by-wire (BBW) systems from Volvo Group Trucks Technology (VGTT), and on an engine management benchmark from Bosch.
\end{abstract}
